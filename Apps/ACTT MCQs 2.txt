Question 2: According to the BONES mnemonic used to assess for ventilation difficulty, which of the following is a recognized risk factor?
A) No beard
B) Young age
C) No teeth
D) BMI under 25
 * Correct Answer: C) No teeth.
   * Reason: The BONES mnemonic highlights factors that make Bag-Valve-Mask (BVM) ventilation difficult: Beard, Obesity (BMI > 26), No teeth, Elderly, and Snoring history. Having no teeth makes it difficult to get a proper mask seal on the patient's face.
 * Incorrect Answers:

Question 3: After placing an advanced airway (like an endotracheal tube), what is the most reliable method for confirming correct tube placement continuously?
A) Auscultating the stomach
B) Observing chest rise
C) Continuous waveform capnography (ETCO2)
D) Checking oxygen saturation on a pulse oximeter
 * Correct Answer: C) Continuous waveform capnography (ETCO2).
   * Reason: Continuous waveform capnography measures the carbon dioxide exhaled by the lungs, providing the most reliable, real-time confirmation that the tube is correctly placed in the trachea and not the esophagus.
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * A) Auscultating the stomach: This is done to rule out esophageal intubation (you shouldn't hear gurgling), but it is not a continuous monitoring method.
   * B) Observing chest rise: While important, chest rise can be misleading or subjective and does not provide objective, continuous confirmation like capnography.
   * D) Checking oxygen saturation: Pulse oximetry measures oxygenation, not ventilation. Oxygen saturation can remain artificially high for several minutes even if the tube is in the wrong place.

   * A) Administer a 500mL fluid bolus immediately: Routine fluid boluses are explicitly not recommended for correcting low urine output in burns.
   * B) Administer an IV diuretic: Diuretics do not fix the underlying hypovolemia causing the low urine output in burn shock.
   * D) Decrease the IV fluid rate by 25%: This action is taken only if the urine output is too high.
Question 4: What is the primary reason hypotonic fluids (such as Lactated Ringer's) should be avoided whenever possible in patients with a Traumatic Brain Injury?

Topic 5: Intraosseous (IO) Access
Question 1: What is the primary indication for establishing intraosseous (IO) access in a trauma patient?
A) Routine administration of maintenance IV fluids
B) Urgent need for vascular access when peripheral IV access is difficult or impossible to obtain
C) To draw blood for routine daily laboratory tests
D) Administration of oral medications in an unconscious patient
 * Correct Answer: B) Urgent need for vascular access when peripheral IV access is difficult or impossible to obtain.
   * Reason: IO access is a rapid, reliable emergency alternative for delivering life-saving fluids and medications when standard intravenous (IV) lines cannot be established quickly.
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * A) Routine administration of maintenance IV fluids: IO access is for emergencies, not routine fluid maintenance, due to the risk of infection and bone complications with prolonged use.
   * C) To draw blood for routine daily laboratory tests: While some labs can be drawn from an IO, it is not placed solely for routine blood draws.
   * D) Administration of oral medications: IO lines are for intravenous-equivalent medications, not oral medications.
Question 2: Which of the following is a commonly preferred anatomical site for IO insertion in an adult patient?
A) Proximal phalanx (finger)
B) Distal radius (wrist)
C) Proximal tibia (just below the knee)
D) Clavicle (collarbone)
 * Correct Answer: C) Proximal tibia (just below the knee).
   * Reason: The proximal tibia is a standard, easily identifiable, and highly accessible site for IO placement, featuring a relatively thin layer of tissue over the bone.
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * A) Proximal phalanx (finger): The bones in the fingers are too small and lack the marrow cavity required for IO fluid resuscitation.
   * B) Distal radius (wrist): This is not a standard IO site; it poses a higher risk of neurovascular injury and is difficult to stabilize.
   * D) Clavicle (collarbone): The clavicle is too close to vital structures (lungs, major vessels) and is not an approved site for IO insertion.
Question 3: Which of the following is an absolute contraindication for placing an IO device in a specific bone?
A) The patient is in hypovolemic shock
B) There is a known or suspected fracture in that specific bone
C) The patient is unconscious
D) The skin over the insertion site is intact and uninjured
 * Correct Answer: B) There is a known or suspected fracture in that specific bone.
   * Reason: Fluid infused into a fractured bone will leak out of the fracture site into the surrounding tissue, leading to extreme swelling (compartment syndrome) rather than entering the central circulation.
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * A) The patient is in hypovolemic shock: This is actually a primary indication for IO placement, not a contraindication.
   * C) The patient is unconscious: Unconsciousness is common in severe trauma and is an indication for aggressive resuscitation, not a contraindication.
   * D) The skin over the insertion site is intact: Intact, uninfected skin is a requirement for safe IO placement.
Question 4: After placing an IO needle, what is a reliable indicator that the needle is correctly positioned in the medullary cavity?
A) The needle feels loose and wobbles easily
B) Severe swelling immediately appears around the insertion site
C) The needle stands firmly on its own and bone marrow can often be aspirated
D) There is absolute resistance when attempting to flush the line
 * Correct Answer: C) The needle stands firmly on its own and bone marrow can often be aspirated.
   * Reason: A properly seated IO needle will feel firmly anchored in the bone. Additionally, aspirating blood/marrow with a syringe confirms placement in the medullary space.
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * A) The needle feels loose and wobbles easily: This indicates the needle has not securely penetrated the bone cortex or has fractured the bone.
   * B) Severe swelling immediately appears: Swelling indicates extravasation (fluid leaking into the tissue), meaning the IO is incorrectly placed or the bone is compromised.
   * D) There is absolute resistance when attempting to flush: While some resistance is normal initially, absolute resistance suggests the needle is clogged or lodged in the solid bone cortex, not the medullary cavity.
Question 5: Why is it standard practice to aggressively flush a newly placed IO line with 10mL of normal saline (often preceded by lidocaine in conscious patients) before starting a continuous infusion?
A) To sterilize the inside of the bone
B) To clear the marrow cavity of thick fibrin and create a space for fluid to flow
C) To lower the patient's heart rate
D) To intentionally fracture the inner bone cortex
 * Correct Answer: B) To clear the marrow cavity of thick fibrin and create a space for fluid to flow.
   * Reason: The medullary space is filled with thick marrow. A rapid flush displaces this marrow, opening the vascular channels so that subsequent fluids and medications can flow easily. (Lidocaine is used to numb the visceral nerve pain inside the bone).
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * A) To sterilize the inside of the bone: Normal saline does not sterilize tissue; sterilization is done on the skin before insertion.
   * C) To lower the patient's heart rate: Saline/lidocaine flushes do not chemically lower the heart rate.
   * D) To intentionally fracture the inner bone cortex: Fracturing the bone is a complication to be avoided, not a goal.
Question 6: Compared to a peripheral IV, how quickly do medications administered via an IO route reach the central circulation?
A) IO administration takes about 30 minutes to take effect
B) IO medications are absorbed as slowly as oral medications
C) IO administration is pharmacokinetically equivalent to IV administration, reaching the heart in seconds
D) IO routes can only be used for fluids, not medications
 * Correct Answer: C) IO administration is pharmacokinetically equivalent to IV administration, reaching the heart in seconds.
   * Reason: The medullary cavity contains a rich network of venous sinusoids that drain directly and rapidly into the central circulation, making IO medication delivery as fast as an IV.
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * A) IO administration takes about 30 minutes: IO delivery is nearly instantaneous, similar to an IV push.
   * B) IO medications are absorbed as slowly as oral medications: Oral meds must pass through the digestive tract; IO meds go directly into the bloodstream.
   * D) IO routes can only be used for fluids: Any medication or blood product that can be given IV can safely be given IO.
Question 7: What is the most severe potential complication associated with leaving an IO needle in place for an extended period (e.g., beyond 24-48 hours)?
A) Osteomyelitis (bone infection)
B) Superficial skin bruising
C) Allergic reaction to the needle material
D) Temporary numbness in the toes
 * Correct Answer: A) Osteomyelitis (bone infection).
   * Reason: The longer an IO device remains in the bone, the higher the risk of introducing bacteria into the marrow cavity, leading to a severe and difficult-to-treat bone infection called osteomyelitis.
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * B) Superficial skin bruising: Bruising is a minor, expected side effect, not a severe complication.
   * C) Allergic reaction to the needle material: Medical-grade IO needles are hypoallergenic; true allergies to the metal are exceptionally rare and not the primary concern.
   * D) Temporary numbness in the toes: While nerve damage is a procedural risk if placed incorrectly, infection is the primary risk of prolonged placement.
Question 8: When preparing the skin for IO insertion, what is the correct procedure?

B) Clean the site with an approved antiseptic (e.g., chlorhexidine or povidone-iodine) and let it dry
C) Wipe the site with a dry cloth to remove dirt
D) Shave the entire limb before applying alcohol
 * Correct Answer: B) Clean the site with an approved antiseptic (e.g., chlorhexidine or povidone-iodine) and let it dry.
   * Reason: Preventing bone infection is critical. The skin must be sterilized with an antiseptic solution before the needle breaks the skin barrier.
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * A) No skin preparation is necessary: Skipping skin prep dramatically increases the risk of introducing pathogens into the bone marrow.
   * C) Wipe the site with a dry cloth: A dry cloth removes gross debris but does not kill bacteria on the skin.
   * D) Shave the entire limb: Shaving the entire limb wastes time and creates micro-abrasions that can harbor bacteria; targeted cleaning is all that is required.

Question 10: During IO infusion, the provider notices the patient's calf becoming hard, swollen, and pale. What does this indicate and what is the immediate action?

Topic 6: Chest Trauma (Needle Thoracostomy)
Question 11: What is the primary, life-threatening condition that needle thoracostomy (needle decompression) is designed to treat in the field?
A) Simple rib fracture
B) Tension pneumothorax
C) Cardiac tamponade
D) Asthma exacerbation
 * Correct Answer: B) Tension pneumothorax.
   * Reason: A tension pneumothorax occurs when air enters the pleural space but cannot escape, building pressure that collapses the lung and eventually shifts the heart, stopping blood return. Needle thoracostomy relieves this trapped air.
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * A) Simple rib fracture: A rib fracture is painful but does not necessarily trap air in the chest; it is treated with analgesia, not a needle.
   * C) Cardiac tamponade: This is fluid in the pericardial sac around the heart, treated with pericardiocentesis, not a pleural needle decompression.
   * D) Asthma exacerbation: Asthma is a lower airway constriction issue treated with bronchodilators, not by puncturing the chest wall.
Question 12: Which combination of clinical signs is the classic presentation of a tension pneumothorax in a trauma patient?
A) High blood pressure, slow heart rate, and irregular breathing
B) Progressive respiratory distress, absent breath sounds on one side, and hypotension (shock)
C) Bilateral wheezing, coughing, and high oxygen saturation
D) Chest pain with equal breath sounds and normal blood pressure
 * Correct Answer: B) Progressive respiratory distress, absent breath sounds on one side, and hypotension (shock).
   * Reason: As pressure builds, the lung collapses (absent breath sounds), breathing becomes difficult (distress), and the vena cava is compressed, reducing blood return to the heart and causing shock (hypotension).
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * A) High blood pressure, slow heart rate: This describes Cushing's Triad, a sign of increased intracranial pressure, not a tension pneumothorax.
   * C) Bilateral wheezing: This indicates an airway issue like asthma or anaphylaxis.
   * D) Chest pain with equal breath sounds: Equal breath sounds suggest the lungs are still functioning and not collapsed by a tension pneumothorax.
Question 13: According to Tactical Combat Casualty Care (TCCC) guidelines, what is the primary anterior anatomical landmark for performing a needle thoracostomy?
A) The 2nd intercostal space at the mid-clavicular line
B) The 5th intercostal space at the mid-axillary line
C) The 4th intercostal space right next to the sternum
D) Directly over the clavicle
 * Correct Answer: A) The 2nd intercostal space at the mid-clavicular line.
   * Reason: The 2nd intercostal space, mid-clavicular line (about 2-3 finger widths below the collarbone, in line with the nipple) is the traditional and primary approved anterior site to access the pleural space safely.
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * B) The 5th intercostal space at the mid-axillary line: This is the approved alternate/lateral site, not the primary anterior site.
   * C) The 4th intercostal space right next to the sternum: Inserting a needle directly next to the sternum risks puncturing the heart or internal mammary arteries.
   * D) Directly over the clavicle: The needle must go between the ribs, not directly into the collarbone.
Question 14: What is the recommended size of the catheter-over-needle used for an adult needle thoracostomy?
A) 22-gauge, 1 inch long
B) 14-gauge or 10-gauge, 3.25 inches long
C) 18-gauge, 1.5 inches long
D) 24-gauge, 0.5 inches long
 * Correct Answer: B) 14-gauge or 10-gauge, 3.25 inches long.
   * Reason: A large-bore (10 or 14-gauge) and long (3.25 inch) needle is required to ensure it is thick enough to vent air rapidly and long enough to penetrate the chest wall muscles of an adult to reach the pleural space.
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * A) 22-gauge, 1 inch long: This is too short to reach the pleural space and too narrow to vent air effectively.
   * C) 18-gauge, 1.5 inches long: This is a standard IV needle size, but frequently fails to penetrate the chest wall deeply enough in trauma patients.
   * D) 24-gauge, 0.5 inches long: This is a pediatric IV needle, completely ineffective for chest decompression.
Question 15: When inserting the needle between the ribs, how should the provider angle/place the needle relative to the rib bones to avoid the neurovascular bundle?
A) Directly under the bottom edge of the upper rib
B) Straight through the center of the rib bone
C) Just over the top edge of the lower rib
D) Angled sharply toward the patient's neck
 * Correct Answer: C) Just over the top edge of the lower rib.
   * Reason: Blood vessels and nerves (the neurovascular bundle) run along the bottom edge of every rib. To avoid lacerating these vessels, the needle must be placed over the top edge of the lower rib in the chosen intercostal space.
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * A) Directly under the bottom edge: This is exactly where the artery, vein, and nerve are located, risking severe bleeding.
   * B) Straight through the center: Attempting to push a needle through solid bone will bend the needle and fail to treat the pneumothorax.
   * D) Angled sharply toward the neck: The needle should be inserted perpendicular (90 degrees) to the chest wall to ensure direct entry into the pleural space.
Question 16: What immediate action should the provider take after successfully placing the needle and removing the sharp trocar?
A) Reassess the patient's breathing, respiratory rate, and hemodynamics
B) Immediately remove the plastic catheter as well
C) Administer CPR regardless of a pulse
D) Pack the needle hub with combat gauze
 * Correct Answer: A) Reassess the patient's breathing, respiratory rate, and hemodynamics.
   * Reason: After decompression, you must immediately reassess the patient to confirm the intervention worked—look for improved breathing, return of radial pulses, or a hiss of escaping air.
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * B) Immediately remove the plastic catheter: The plastic catheter must be left in place to continue venting air until a chest tube can be placed by a surgeon.
   * C) Administer CPR: If the tension pneumothorax was the cause of shock and decompression is successful, the patient will regain blood pressure; CPR is only for cardiac arrest.
   * D) Pack the needle hub: Packing the hub would block the air from escaping, recreating the tension pneumothorax.
Question 17: If a provider misses the mid-clavicular line and places the anterior needle too close to the sternum (medially), what is the most significant risk?
A) Lacerating the liver
B) Puncturing the heart or great vessels
C) Damaging the spinal cord

Question 18: What is a defining characteristic that differentiates a tension pneumothorax from a simple pneumothorax?
A) The presence of chest pain
B) Shortness of breath
C) Hemodynamic compromise (shock/hypotension)
D) The fact that it was caused by trauma
 * Correct Answer: C) Hemodynamic compromise (shock/hypotension).
   * Reason: Both cause chest pain and shortness of breath. However, a tension pneumothorax creates so much pressure that it crushes the vena cava, stopping blood flow to the heart and causing shock. A simple pneumothorax does not cause this cardiovascular collapse.
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * A) The presence of chest pain: Both simple and tension pneumothoraces cause chest pain.

B) Perform a second needle decompression adjacent to the first one or at the alternate lateral site
C) Remove the original catheter and cover the hole with a standard bandage
D) Start chest compressions
 * Correct Answer: B) Perform a second needle decompression adjacent to the first one or at the alternate lateral site.
   * Reason: The original catheter has likely clotted off or kinked, allowing tension to rebuild. The correct action is to perform another decompression using a new needle to re-vent the trapped air.
 * Incorrect Answers:
   * A) Administer an opioid: Opioids depress the respiratory drive and will not fix the mechanical pressure collapsing the lung.
   * C) Remove the original catheter: Removing the catheter removes the only vent; you should leave it and place a second one.
   * D) Start chest compressions: Unless the patient has lost their pulse entirely (cardiac arrest), compressions are not indicated.
